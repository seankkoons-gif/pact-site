Pact: A Legal Overview for General Counsel

How autonomous agent activity becomes auditable, verifiable, and attributable.

The Problem (Plain English)

Autonomous software agents can now negotiate, transact, and execute actions without human intervention. When something goes wrong, traditional logs and dashboards are insufficient: they are mutable, operator-controlled, and not designed to assign responsibility.

General Counsel are left with incomplete records and unclear accountability.

What Pact Is

Pact is a system for generating cryptographic evidence of autonomous agent negotiations and transactions.

It produces sealed evidence bundles ("Auditor Packs") that can be independently verified offline and reviewed without trusting the system that generated them.

Pact does not enforce outcomes or resolve disputes. It produces proof.

What Pact Produces

Each Pact transaction can produce:

  • A complete, hash-linked transcript of negotiation rounds
  • Signed identities for participating agents
  • Records of policy checks and settlement attempts
  • A verification report confirming integrity and compliance
  • A signed responsibility attribution ("Default Blame Logic")
  • A GC-ready PDF and insurer summary

All artifacts are portable and verifiable without access to Pact infrastructure.

Responsibility Attribution (Important)

Pact uses deterministic Default Blame Logic to identify:

  • A responsible actor, or
  • NO_FAULT when appropriate

This attribution is based solely on signed evidence and protocol rules, not human judgment or subjective interpretation.

Pact does not determine damages, remedies, or enforcement actions.

What Pact Is Not

Pact is not:

  • A marketplace
  • An escrow provider
  • An arbitration system
  • A monitoring dashboard
  • A decision-maker

It is an evidence standard.

Why This Matters to Legal Teams

With Pact:

  • Evidence can be reviewed in minutes, not weeks
  • Responsibility is provable, not debated
  • Records remain valid even if vendors, platforms, or systems change
  • Insurers and auditors receive artifacts they can independently verify

If your organization allows autonomous systems to spend money or commit resources, Pact ensures you can prove what happened — and who was responsible.
